Nigerian Financial Regulations Rules

Comprehensive Guide for Public Servants

Introduction to Financial Regulations Rules

i. Purpose of Financial Regulations

The Financial Regulations (FR) of the Federal Government of Nigeria serve as the primary legal framework guiding financial management and control in public service institutions. It is a compilation of rules, procedures, and responsibilities designed to promote accountability, transparency, efficiency, and discipline in the handling of public resources.

This comprehensive guide covers Chapters 7 to 32 of the Nigerian Financial Regulations, which provide detailed guidelines for the management of public funds, assets, and financial operations across all government ministries, departments, and agencies.

Overview of Key Areas Covered:

  • Financial Operations: Bank accounts, cash management, and payment procedures
  • Asset Management: Stores, vehicles, and government property
  • Personnel Finance: Salaries, pensions, and advances
  • Controls & Safeguards: Internal audit, stock verification, and loss prevention
  • Procurement & Contracts: Public procurement processes and contract management
  • Compliance & Enforcement: Offences, sanctions, and regulatory compliance

ii. Legal Backing

Legal Backing

The Financial Regulations derive their authority from the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria, the Finance (Control and Management) Act, and other related financial laws and circulars.

iii. Scope

Scope

The Public Service Rules are founded on several key principles:

  • The Regulations apply to: All ministries, departments, and agencies (MDAs).
  • Officers involved in revenue collection, expenditure management, and accounting.
  • Parastatals operating under government financial supervision.

iv. Importance of the Financial Regulations

Importance of the Financial Regulations

  • Ensures proper documentation and management of public funds.
  • Prevents financial misconduct and misappropriation.
  • Strengthens internal control and audit processes.
  • Guides public officers in maintaining fiscal discipline.

v. Structure

Structure

The Gazette is divided into 32 chapters, each addressing a core area of financial administration such as:

  • Revenue collection
  • Payment procedures
  • Bank operations
  • Procurement and stores management
  • Salaries and pensions
  • Public audit and survey processes
  • Codification of offences and penalties

1Financial Authorities and Responsibilities of Public Officers

Overview

This chapter outlines the various financial roles and responsibilities of public officers in the Federal Government. It sets the tone for accountability by clearly stating who is responsible for what in the financial ecosystem of government institutions.

Key Points

i. Accounting Officers

  • Permanent Secretaries or Heads of Extra-Ministerial Departments are designated as Accounting Officers.
  • They are responsible for the control and management of public funds and must ensure that financial regulations are observed.

ii. Responsibilities of Accounting Officers

  • Ensure all expenditure is properly authorized and budgeted.
  • Maintain accurate accounting records.
  • Safeguard government assets and resources.
  • Establish and maintain internal control systems.

iii. Delegation of Authority

  • While responsibilities may be delegated, the accountability remains with the Accounting Officer.
  • Delegated duties must be accompanied by clear written instructions.

iv. Financial Duties of Other Officers

  • Heads of units, departmental finance officers, and storekeepers also have specific duties assigned to them.
  • They are responsible for accurate reporting, safe custody of funds and items, and compliance with rules.

v. Sanctions for Financial Misconduct

  • Public officers who mismanage funds or violate financial rules are liable to disciplinary action, including surcharge, demotion, dismissal, or prosecution.

Important Note: The rules in this chapter establish the legal basis for all subsequent provisions and should be read in conjunction with the Constitution of the Federal Republic of Nigeria and other relevant statutes governing public service administration.

2Revenue Collection and Accounting

Overview

This chapter outlines the procedures and controls governing how government revenue is to be collected, recorded, and accounted for. It emphasizes transparency, proper documentation, and accountability in revenue processes across Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs).

Key Points

i. Revenue Officers

  • Officers authorized to collect government revenue must be formally designated.
  • They are responsible for safe custody, prompt lodgement, and accurate accounting of revenue.

ii. Use of Government Treasury Receipt (GTR)

  • Every revenue collection must be receipted using Treasury-printed and numbered GTRs.
  • Duplicate copies are retained and submitted for accounting.

iii. Revenue Head Codes

  • Each type of revenue must be assigned to its correct subhead or code.
  • Misclassification is considered a violation of the regulations.

iv. Timely Lodgement

  • All revenue collected must be lodged into designated government accounts not later than the next working day.
  • Failure to lodge promptly is regarded as fraudulent conduct.

iv. Remittance Procedures

  • MDAs without bank branches nearby must follow special remittance instructions from the Office of the Accountant-General.

iv. Revenue Books and Registers

  • Proper Revenue Cash Books and Revenue Registers must be maintained.
  • Revenue entries must be updated daily, showing GTR number, amount, payer, and date.

iv. Reconciliation and Reporting

  • Periodic bank reconciliation and monthly revenue returns must be rendered to appropriate authorities.

iv. Sanctions for Revenue Violations

  • Officers who divert revenue, delay lodgement, or fail to issue GTRs are subject to disciplinary action, including suspension, surcharge, or dismissal.

3Expenditure: Authorities for Recurrent and Capital Expenditures

Overview

This chapter provides the rules and limits governing the authorization of government spending, distinguishing between recurrent (routine) and capital (developmental) expenditures. It ensures that all spending is in line with budgetary provisions and backed by proper approvals.

Key Points

i. Definition of Expenditures

  • Recurrent Expenditure: Routine operational costs like salaries, utilities, and supplies.
  • Capital Expenditure: Investments in assets such as buildings, roads, and equipment.

ii. Expenditure Must Be Authorized

  • All spending must be approved via Annual Appropriation Acts, Supplementary Appropriations, or Virement Warrants.
  • No officer may incur any expenditure unless there is a legal appropriation and authority to do so.

iii. Vote Book Maintenance

  • MDAs must maintain a Vote Book to track spending against budgetary allocations.
  • Entries must be up-to-date and reflect actual commitments and balances available.

iv. Expenditure Limits

  • Certain categories of officers have financial limits they must not exceed without special approval.
  • Exceeding these limits is a serious offence.

v. Control of Excess Votes

  • No expenditure should be incurred if it will result in an excess vote.
  • Supplementary approval must be obtained before any excess is incurred.

vi. Use of Warrants

  • Recurrent and capital expenditures are authorized through specific warrants issued by the Ministry of Finance.
  • These include General, Supplementary, Virement, and Advance Warrants.

vii. Role of the Budget Office

  • The Budget Office must monitor compliance, track vote usage, and report anomalies.
  • It also issues Release Warrants to MDAs.

viii. Sanctions for Unauthorized Expenditures

  • Spending without approval, incurring excess votes, or misapplying funds may lead to disciplinary action, surcharge, or criminal prosecution.

4Expenditure: Classification and Control

Overview

This chapter explains the proper classification of public expenditure and the internal controls that must be in place to ensure budget discipline, transparency, and the lawful use of public funds. It emphasizes the need to match each expense to its appropriate category and budget line.

Key Points

i. Expenditure Classification

  • Expenditure must be classified into Recurrent or Capital, and further into Personal Emoluments (PE), Overheads, or Capital Projects.
  • Each expenditure must be linked to the correct vote subhead and code to avoid misapplication.

ii. Budgetary Control

  • Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs) must strictly adhere to the limits provided in the budget.
  • Every approving officer must verify the availability of funds before approving any payment.

iii. Commitment Control

  • MDAs must establish Commitment Control Registers to track financial obligations as they arise.
  • No commitment should be made without prior approval or availability of funds.

iv. Use of Vote Book

  • A properly maintained Vote Book is essential for tracking spending.
    It shows:
    • Approved amount
    • Actual commitment
    • Balance remaining

v. Internal Control Mechanisms

  • MDAs must establish and follow internal control systems for financial operations, including:
    • Segregation of duties (e.g., between approval and payment)
    • Regular reconciliations
    • Supervisory checks

vi. Role of the Head of Accounts

  • Responsible for ensuring:
    • That expenditure aligns with budgetary classifications.
    • That proper authorization procedures are followed.
    • That records are accurate and up to date.

vii. Unauthorised Transfers Between Votes

  • Transferring funds between subheads without due approval is prohibited.
  • All virement must be backed by formal approval from the appropriate authority.

viii. Sanctions for Misclassification or Control Failure

  • Errors in classification or failure to implement controls can result in:
    • Query
    • Surcharge
    • Disciplinary action including dismissal or prosecution

5Standardization and Accounting Procedures

Overview

This chapter outlines the standard accounting procedures to be followed by all Ministries, Departments, and Agencies (MDAs) to ensure uniformity, accuracy, and transparency in public financial management. It also sets the framework for the preparation and presentation of government financial statements.

Key Points

i. Uniform Accounting Standards

  • All public financial records must follow standard formats prescribed by the Office of the Accountant-General of the Federation (OAGF).
  • These standards ensure consistency across all MDAs

ii. Chart of Accounts

  • MDAs must use the approved chart of accounts to classify income and expenditure items.
  • This helps in aggregation and national reporting.

iii. Accrual and Cash Basis

  • Nigeria predominantly uses cash basis accounting, but select aspects of accrual basis (like commitments) are incorporated for improved tracking.

iv. Source Documents

  • Every accounting entry must be backed by a source document, e.g.:
      • Payment voucher
    • Receipt
    • Invoice
    • Contract agreement

v. Daily and Monthly Records

  • Cash books, ledgers, and registers must be:
    • Updated daily
    • Balanced and reconciled monthly

vi. Reconciliation

  • Bank reconciliation statements must be prepared monthly.
  • Differences between bank statements and books must be investigated promptly.

vii. Audit Trail and Security

  • All accounting records must provide a clear audit trail.
  • Records must be kept secure and accessible for audit and verification.

viii. Automation of Records

  • Government encourages the use of automated accounting systems where possible, e.g., GIFMIS (Government Integrated Financial Management Information System).

ix. Retention of Records

  • Financial records must be retained for a minimum of 6 years, unless specified otherwise by law or regulation.

x. Sanctions for Non-Compliance

  • Any failure to follow accounting procedures, or falsification of records, attracts penalties including:
    • Disciplinary action
    • Surcharge
    • Prosecution

6Payment Procedures

Overview

Chapter 6 outlines the official procedures for processing payments within the public service. It ensures that all government disbursements are lawful, verified, approved, and properly documented, maintaining accountability and reducing the risk of financial misconduct.

Key Points

i. Payment Authorization

  • No payment shall be made without:
    • A duly authorized payment voucher (PV)
    • Approval by a competent officer
    • Budgetary provision and cash backing

ii. Supporting Documentation

  • Every PV must be supported by original documents such as:
    • Invoice
    • Receipt
    • Delivery note
    • Certificate of completion (for projects)

iii. Preparation of Payment Vouchers

  • PVs must:
    • Be serially numbered
    • State the vote (budget) head
    • Clearly describe the service
    • Bear the signature of the authorized officer

iv. Payment Methods

  • Payments may be made via:
    • E-payment systems (preferable for transparency)
    • Cheques (in special circumstances)
    • Cash (only in limited and approved situations)

v. Cheque Payments

  • Cheques must:
    • Be signed by two authorized signatories
    • Be recorded in a cheque register
    • Be accompanied by PV and supporting docs

vi. E-Payment Adoption

  • MDAs are encouraged to process payments via the GIFMIS platform or other government-approved electronic payment platforms to:
    • Reduce fraud
    • Increase traceability

vii. Personal Responsibility of Certifying Officers

  • Officers who certify payments are personally liable for any loss resulting from wrongful payment.

viii. Prompt Payment

  • Payments must be made within a reasonable time after goods/services are delivered and verified.
  • Delays must be justified and documented.

ix. Duplicate and Over-payment

  • Duplicate or over-payment must be reported immediately.
  • Recovery processes must be initiated without delay.

x. Sanctions

  • Irregular or unauthorized payments may lead to:
    • Recovery (surcharge)
    • Query or disciplinary action
    • Prosecution, depending on severity

7Bank Accounts and Cheques

Overview

This chapter focuses on the management of government bank accounts and the use of cheques. It outlines procedures to ensure transparency, proper control, and accountability in the handling of public funds deposited in banks.

Key Points

i. Opening of Bank Accounts

  • Only the Accountant-General of the Federation (AGF) can authorize the opening of official government bank accounts.
  • MDAs must not open or operate bank accounts without AGFâ€™s approval.

ii. Types of Bank Accounts

  • Main Treasury Accounts (for central government transactions)
  • Project Accounts (for specific funded projects)
  • Revenue Collection Accounts
  • Imprest Accounts (for petty expenses)

Attention

All accounts must serve clearly defined purposes.

iii. Bank Signatories

  • Government accounts must have at least two signatories.
  • Signatories must be approved officers, and combinations must be defined for signing authority.

iv. Bank Mandate Forms

  • Each account must have a duly completed bank mandate specifying:
    • Account purpose
    • Signatories and their specimen signatures
    • Limits for each officerâ€™s approval

v. Cheque Books

  • Must be securely stored and controlled.
  • Each cheque used must be recorded in a cheque register.
  • Cheques should be serially numbered and used in order.

vi. Preparation and Signing of Cheques

  • Cheques must only be issued against duly authorized Payment Vouchers (PVs).
  • No blank cheques or post-dated cheques are allowed.
  • Cheques must be:
    • Correctly written
    • Properly signed
    • Accompanied by complete documentation

vii. Reconciliation

  • Monthly bank reconciliation must be performed by MDAs.
  • Discrepancies must be reported and resolved promptly.

viii. Use of E-Banking

  • While cheques are still valid, MDAs are encouraged to use e-payment systems for improved efficiency and audit trail.

ix. Closure of Bank Accounts

  • If an account is no longer required, it must be closed with AGF's approval.
  • Final balances must be transferred to a designated account, and unused cheques canceled and returned.

x. Sanctions

  • Unauthorized bank account operations, improper use of cheques, or failure to reconcile accounts may lead to:
    • oSurcharge
    • Disciplinary measures
    • Criminal prosecution

8Cash Book and Monthly Accounts

Overview

This chapter provides the rules for maintaining Cash Books and preparing Monthly Accounts in MDAs (Ministries, Departments, and Agencies). It ensures all cash transactions are accurately recorded, balanced, and reported in a timely and transparent manner.

Key Points

Cash Book Maintenance

  • Each MDA must keep a Cash Book to record:
    • All receipts (revenue and other inflows)
    • All payments (authorized expenditures)
    • Entries must be made daily and be chronologically ordered.

Structure of the Cash Book

  • Separate columns for:
    • Date
    • Particulars (description)
    • Receipt number
    • Payment voucher number
    • Amount (debit/credit)
    • Balance

Accuracy and Timeliness

  • Transactions must be entered:
    • On the day they occur
    • Using approved formats and ink
    • Alterations must not be made with correction fluidâ€”cancel by drawing a line and initialing.

Balancing the Cash Book

  • The Cash Book must be balanced daily.
  • The closing balance must agree with the actual cash on hand or bank balance.

Cash Book Certification

  • Must be certified by the Head of Accounts or designated officer monthly.
  • Certification includes:
    • Checking entries for accuracy
    • Verifying supporting documents
    • Ensuring correct balances

Monthly Returns

  • Monthly accounting returns must be:
    • Prepared at the end of each month
    • Submitted to the Office of the Accountant-General
    • Include summaries of revenue, expenditure, and cash positions

Subsidiary Registers

  • Where applicable, maintain subsidiary records such as:
    • Revenue registers
    • Expenditure ledgers

Vote books

  • These must reconcile with the Cash Book monthly.

Verification and Audit

  • Internal and external auditors will verify Cash Book entries.
  • Discrepancies must be addressed and corrected immediately.

Security and Retention

  • Cash Books must be kept securely.
  • Retention period is usually at least 7 years, as per records management regulations.

Sanctions

  • Failure to maintain or reconcile the Cash Book may result in:
    • Queries
    • Surcharges
    • Disciplinary action

9Adjustments

Overview

This chapter addresses the procedures for adjustments in government accounts. Adjustments are necessary to correct errors, reclassify expenditures or revenues, and ensure the accuracy and integrity of financial records.

Definition of Adjustment:

An adjustment refers to any correction made in financial records to ensure proper classification and reporting of transactions.
These include:
  • Transfers between accounts
  • Correction of erroneous postings
  • Reallocation of expenditures or revenues

Key Points

i. Authority for Adjustment

  • No adjustment should be made without the written approval of the Head of Accounts or a duly authorized officer.
  • All adjustments must be supported with valid documentation.

Adjustment Journal Voucher (AJV)

  • Adjustments must be recorded using a specific form called the Adjustment Journal Voucher.
  • The AJV must include:
    • Reference to the original entry
    • Nature and reason for the adjustment
    • Amount involved
    • Authorizing officer's signature

Timing for Adjustments

  • Adjustments must be made in the same accounting period where the error was discovered.
  • Delays can distort monthly or annual financial statements.

Types of Common Adjustments

  • oReposting of expenditure to correct vote head
  • Correction of duplicated entries
  • Rectification of over- or under-stated balances
  • Adjustments arising from audit findings

Impact on Financial Reports

  • Adjustments must be accurately reflected in:
    • Cash Book
    • Trial Balance
    • Monthly and Annual Financial Statements

Adjustment Register

  • All adjustments must be entered into an Adjustment Register for audit trail and future reference.

Monitoring and Controls

  • Frequent or large adjustments are subject to investigation.
  • Pattern of errors may indicate poor internal control or fraud.

Audit Review

  • Adjustment records are reviewed during internal and external audits.
  • Unsupported or unauthorized adjustments can lead to sanctions.

Sanctions

  • Adjustments without proper authority or documentation attract penalties such as:
    • Queries
    • Surcharges
    • Disciplinary actions

10Imprest

Overview

This chapter outlines the rules and procedures for the issuance, utilization, retirement, and control of imprests in government financial operations. Imprests are advance funds given to public officers to meet urgent or minor official expenses.

Key Points

i. Definition of Imprest

  • An imprest is a temporary advance of funds provided to officers for specific purposes.
  • It is meant for routine, petty, or emergency expenses which cannot be handled through normal payment procedures.

ii. Types of Imprest

  • Standing Imprest: Given for recurring official needs (e.g., transport, postage).
  • Special/Ad hoc Imprest: Issued for one-off events or assignments (e.g., field trips, workshops).

iii. Approval and Authorization

  • Imprest must be approved by the Accounting Officer or Head of Accounts.
  • Limits and thresholds are set depending on the nature of the activity and the officer's designation.

iv. Utilization Rules

  • Must only be used for official expenses.
  • Personal or unauthorized use is strictly prohibited.
  • Receipts and vouchers must be collected for every expenditure.

v. Retirement of Imprest

  • Retirement means the settlement or accounting of the imprest.
  • Must be done immediately after the assignment or within the prescribed timeframe (usually 7 to 14 days).
  • Submit:
    • Detailed statement of expenditure
    • Original receipts and vouchers
    • Balance of unspent funds (if any)

vi. Unretired Imprest

  • Officers who fail to retire imprests on time:
    • Will be denied new imprests
    • May face salary deductions
    • Are liable to disciplinary action

vii. Control Measures

  • Imprests should be minimized and used only when necessary.
  • Maintain an Imprest Register showing:
    • Date of issue
    • Amount
    • Purpose
    • Officerâ€™s name
    • Retirement date

viii. Audit Trail

  • All imprests and retirement documents must be available for audit.
  • Missing documents or misuse can lead to surcharges or criminal liability.

ix. Accountability

  • The officer collecting the imprest is personally accountable for the funds.
  • Transfers to third parties without authorization are not allowed.

Sanctions

  • Non-retirement or misuse of imprest attracts:
    • Queries
    • Surcharge
    • Suspension of further advances
    • Dismissal in severe cases

11Custody of Public Moneys, Stamps, Security Books and Documents

Overview

Chapter 11 deals with the proper custody and protection of public funds, revenue stamps, and sensitive government documents. It emphasizes security, accountability, and preventing losses or fraud in handling these assets.

Key Points

i. Custody of Public Funds

  • Public funds must be kept securely in government-approved safes or strong rooms.
  • Only authorized officers should have access.
  • Cash must not be left unattended or held in private homes.

ii. Segregation of Duties

  • Officers responsible for receiving, holding, and disbursing public funds should not perform accounting or recording duties simultaneously.
  • This ensures internal control and reduces the risk of fraud.

iii. Revenue Stamps and Stamp Duties

  • Treated as cash equivalents.
  • Must be stored securely and recorded in a Stamp Register.
  • Daily reconciliation of stamp stock is mandatory.

iv. Security Books and Receipts

  • Includes cheque books, receipt books, licenses, permits, and other accountable documents.
  • These must be locked away and issued under strict control.
  • Spoiled or cancelled documents must be preserved for audit purposes.

v. Register of Security Documents

  • Must record all serial-numbered security books issued and returned.
  • Unauthorized printing or reproduction of such documents is prohibited and punishable.

vi. Monthly Checks and Balancing

  • Heads of Departments must conduct monthly checks on cash, stamps, and books in custody.
  • Reports of these checks should be documented and signed.

vii. Cash Limits

  • Officers must not hold cash beyond approved limits.
  • Excess cash must be deposited into government bank accounts promptly.

viii. Loss or Theft

  • Any loss, theft, or suspected fraud involving funds, stamps, or documents must be reported immediately.
  • A Board of Inquiry or Police investigation may be instituted.
  • Officers found negligent will be surcharged and may face disciplinary action.

ix. Safety and Insurance

  • Government premises must be equipped with safes, locks, and where necessary, insurance against burglary or fire.

x. Sanctions

  • Failure to comply with custody rules can lead to:
    • Queries
    • Suspensions
    • Criminal prosecution
    • Recovery of lost funds from the officerâ€™s salary

12Receipt and Licence Books

Overview

Chapter 12 provides clear guidelines on the issuance, control, and accountability of receipt books and licence books, which are crucial instruments in government revenue collection. The chapter ensures these tools are securely handled, properly documented, and auditable at all times.

Key Points

i. Issue and Control

  • Receipt and licence books are classified as security documents.
  • Their issue is managed by designated officers and must be recorded in a Register of Security Documents.
  • Each book must be serially numbered.

ii. Proper Custody

  • Must be locked and secured when not in use.
  • Access limited to authorized officers only.
  • Unused and completed books are kept under tight control.

iii. Use of Official Receipts Only

  • Only government-approved receipt and licence books are valid for official use.
  • All monies received must be acknowledged with official numbered receipts issued from the authorized book.

iv. Recording and Balancing

  • Officers must balance their receipt books daily and account for all transactions.
  • Duplicates/counterfoils must be retained for audit purposes.
  • No receipts should be issued on loose sheets or unofficial papers.

v. Spoilt or Cancelled Receipts

  • Spoiled or cancelled receipts must not be destroyed.
  • They are to be crossed out, marked as "Cancelled", and preserved intact in the book.

vi. Return of Used Books

  • Used-up books must be returned to the issuing authority and registered as "Returned".
  • Departments must maintain records of books in use, books returned, and stock balance.

vii. Stock Verification

  • Periodic checks on receipt and licence books must be conducted by Heads of Departments or internal auditors.
  • Any discrepancies are to be investigated immediately.

viii. Loss of Receipt or Licence Book

  • Loss must be reported immediately.
  • May result in a Board of Inquiry or disciplinary action.
  • Officer responsible may be held liable for revenue unaccounted for due to the loss.

ix. Unauthorised Printing Prohibited

  • It is a serious offence to print, order, or use receipt or licence books outside official channels.

x. Sanctions

  • Violations of these rules may lead to:
    • Query
    • Surcharge
    • Suspension
    • Dismissal
    • Criminal prosecution depending on the gravity of the offence

13Deposits

Professional Examinations

This chapter outlines the procedures, control, and accountability related to deposits made into government accounts. Deposits refer to monies temporarily held by the government, which may be refundable or meant for specific services. This chapter ensures deposits are accurately recorded, properly authorized, and regularly reviewed to avoid misuse or loss.

Key Points

i. Deposits

  • Deposits include:
    • Earnest money from contractors
    • Security deposits
    • Caution fees
    • Court deposits
    • Unclaimed salaries or pensions
    • Other temporary funds held by MDAs

ii. Deposit Register

  • Each MDA (Ministry, Department, or Agency) must maintain a Deposit Register showing:
    • Name of depositor
    • Date
    • Amount
    • Purpose
    • Date of refund or transfer

Please Note:

Entries must be sequential and up-to-date.

iii. Proper Classification

  • Deposits must be correctly classified and coded under the right deposit accounts in the chart of accounts.

iv. Receipt and Acknowledgement

  • An official receipt must be issued for every deposit.
  • Receipts must include details of the deposit purpose.

v. Accounting for Deposits

  • Deposits are not treated as revenue.
  • They are liabilities and must be shown as such in the accounts.

vi. Withdrawal and Refund

  • Refunds can only be made with proper authority and documentation.
  • Unclaimed deposits should be transferred to revenue after six years if not claimed, in line with Financial Regulations.

vii. Transfer and Adjustments

  • Deposits can be transferred between accounts only with proper approval.
  • Any adjustment must be supported by written evidence.

viii. Custody and Security

  • Deposit records and instruments (e.g., bonds, bank guarantees) must be securely stored.
  • Only authorized officers should access or handle deposit matters.

ix. Reconciliation and Verification

  • Periodic reconciliation of deposit balances must be carried out.
  • Internal audit units must verify deposit balances and usage regularly.

x. Reporting and Oversight

  • Heads of Accounts and Directors of Finance must review deposits monthly.
  • Any irregularities must be promptly investigated and resolved.

xi. Sanctions and Penalties

  • Misappropriation or mismanagement of deposits can lead to:
    • Surcharge
    • Disciplinary actions
    • Criminal prosecution
    • Officers failing to maintain deposit records properly may be liable for any losses.

14Advances

Overview

This chapter deals with the guidelines for granting, recording, controlling, and recovering advances in government financial management. Advances refer to temporary financial assistance provided to public officers or government establishments for approved purposes, such as personal, motor vehicle, or housing loans, as well as operational advances for official duties.

Key Points

i. Conditions for Granting Advances

  • Advances must be duly authorized by the Accounting Officer or designated authority.
  • Applicants must be confirmed staff (for personal loans) and must provide collateral or guarantors where required.
  • A written request and a clear purpose must accompany all applications.

ii. Repayment Terms

  • Repayment must be scheduled through monthly deductions from salaries or other entitlements.
  • Period for repayment must not exceed the tenure of service or contract.
  • Early repayment is allowed and encouraged.

iii. Advance Ledger and Records

  • A personal advance ledger must be maintained for each officer granted an advance.
  • The ledger should detail:
    • Date of advance
    • Purpose
    • Amount
    • Repayment schedule
    • Balance outstanding

iv. Responsibility of the Accountant

  • Must ensure timely deductions and accurate records.
  • Must issue reminders for overdue advances.
  • Must reconcile advance ledgers monthly.

v. Recovery from Emoluments

  • In the event of transfer, retirement, or resignation, any outstanding advances must be recovered in full from final entitlements.
  • Unrecovered amounts become surchargeable to the responsible officer.

v. Interest on Advances

  • Certain advances (e.g., vehicle or housing loans) may attract interest as prescribed by government policy.
  • Interest rates must be clearly stated in the approval letter.

vi. Advances Not to Be Treated as Expenditure

  • Advances are assets, not expenditures, and must be treated as receivables in the accounts.

vii. Default and Sanctions

Key Points

ix. Sanctions and Penalties

    • Withdrawal of status

    • Adherence to government financial policies is required.

    ix. Importance

    • These guidelines help ensure that parastatals operate transparently, responsibly, and in line with national financial policies.

    Conclusion

    The Nigerian Public Service Rules represent a comprehensive framework designed to ensure the effective and efficient operation of the civil service. By providing clear guidelines on all aspects of public service administration, the rules help maintain order, discipline, and professionalism in the service.

    These rules balance the rights of public servants with their responsibilities to the nation. They establish mechanisms for fair treatment, due process, and professional development while demanding high standards of integrity and performance.

    All public servants are expected to familiarize themselves with these rules and adhere to their provisions in the discharge of their official duties. The successful implementation of these rules requires commitment from both leadership and staff at all levels to uphold the principles of service, integrity, and national development.

    As Nigeria continues to develop and face new governance challenges, these rules provide a stable foundation for public administration while allowing for necessary adaptations through established amendment procedures.
